Nirvana

~ Cadet Mahee, 2166



It’s 4pm already. Rafiqul Islam is still waiting for Sagar to bring the files that require his urgent signature. Boss has called him three times already to sign and send the files as quickly as possible. Rafiq was born in a poor family and worked very hard to climb the hierarchy of posts to reach his present position. Habitually, he becomes very uncomfortable when a task is not done in time. Today, he is a bit sick. So, he’s staying at his home.

After 5 minutes, the calling bell rang. Rafiqul Islam hurriedly opened the door to find Sagar, standing with a file in his hand. He seems non-chalant compared to his usual self. Sagar started with a salam:

        Sagar: Assalamualaikum, sir. How are you?
        Rafiq: I’m Alhamdulillah fine, just a bit feverish.
        Sagar: May you get well soon, sir.
        Rafiq: Thanks. Have you brought the files?
        Sagar: Yes, sir.
        Rafiq: Then give it to me. Boss is very angry for the delay.

Sagar did not show any interest in giving the files to Rafiq. Instead, he ignored the comment totally and asked,
        Sagar: How are your family member, sir?
        Rafiq: Well, they are fine.
        Sagar: That’s good to hear.
        Rafiq: Just hand over the files to me, will you?

Sagar’s eyes rolled around the room for a bit. Then he looked dead into Rafiq’s eyes and said,
        Sagar: Why are you so much into these mundane matters, sir? What good does it bring to you?
        Rafiq: What are you trying to imply?
        Sagar: Just think about it, sir. We both are working in the office from day to day. What is that actually?
        Rafiq: You’ve gone mad, Sagar. I need income to run my family and my work benefits many people.
        Sagar: What do you think a family is? Would you care for women or children whom you don’t know? Would you spend the whole day to earn bread for them? Answer honestly sir.
        Rafiq: Well… no. but a family is a different….
        Sagar: Different thing? Before you married your wife, did you know her? You could have never met her or never have married. It was purely by chance that you two are married. Except the marriage part, your wife is no one different more important than any other human in the world. Neither are your children.

Rafiq was dumbfounded. The conclusion is logical, but not acceptable. Something just isn’t right about the argument. He said in a week voice,
        Rafiq: Just give me the file. I am feeling sick hearing your nihilistic thoughts.
        Sagar: Sir, your work is meaningless. If you resign from your post, another person will be appointed in your place within a few days. That’s how much you matter to the company. It’s you who are in a delusion that you matter. This feeling is what keeps you bound to your family and workplace. You are nothing more than a prisoner in a chaotic world, shackled by this delusion.

Rafiq couldn’t bear it anymore. He shouted like a maniac, “GET OUT MY HOUSE!” there was no sign of Sagar trying to ‘get out’ from there. Instead, he smiled and said,
        Sagar: I will, sir. But that would be after I free you from your voluntary enslavement in this world.
        Rafiq: What would that be?
        Sagar: I’ll tell you, but before that, answer the call that is just about to come.
        Rafiq: How do you know…...?

Rafiq’s phone started ringing. It was Rafiq’s favourite ringtone- two birds continuously ringing. But at that moment, if felt like the roar of an unknown monster. Rafiq was sweating vehemently. He saw the name of Kamrul, his most favourite junior co-worker on the display of his phone. Rafiq accepted the call.
        Rafiq: Hello, Kamrul!
Kamrul’s voice was shaking.
        Sagar: Sir, Sagar is dead sir.
        Rafiq: What are you saying!
        Sagar: Yes sir! He died in a road accident while going to your house.
        Rafiq: But…… But he is sitting right in front of……

He couldn’t say anything more. The phone dropped down from his hand. He yelled,”Afroza! Afroza!” but his wife, Afroza was at her paternal home with her son. The yelling echoed throughout the empty house. Sagar threw a monstrous smile at Rafiq.
        Sagar: Let’s talk about how to achieve freedom, shall we?
        Rafiq: (Silence)
        Sagar: Die. Death is what frees you from it all. Die, sir. Your company will publish a condolence note, your family will be in tears for some days. But let the days pass, and you’ll be forgotten. The forgotten have no responsibilities, nothing to think of. Just addictive emptiness will engulf you, bit by bit. Don’t you think that’s what nirvana is?

Afroza Khanam is crying. Her Husband, Rafiqul Islam was found hanging from the ceiling fan. It’s unclear why he would commit suicide like that. Kamrul was standing beside her, with a stiff face. His lips did not utter any words. His head was still trying to process the memories of the day he last talked with Rafiq.


When the phone fell from Rafiq’s hand, the call was still going on. Kamrul heard the voice of Rafiq and Sagar, discussing about death. “……Don’t you think that’s what Nirvana is?” Rafiq stayed silent for a second and then said boldly,
        Rafiq: I don’t know who are you, or what you are. But if that’s what you think a human should do, to die and free himself from the earth, then I don’t agree with you. Listen, I have only one life, one chance in this universe to be alive. No matter how much illogical it is, I won’t let it slide.
        Sagar: (Ominous laughter) It seems it’s impossible to persuade you to do it yourself.
        Rafiq: What do you mean?
        Sagar: Remember what I said earlier? I have come to free you from your voluntary enslavement. What gave you the impression that you have any choice here? You don’t have any choice, like the one’s body I possess right now.
        Rafiq: What are you? The devil’s advocate?
        Sagar: Umm…… no. don’t you think my good deeds make me an angel? I like to consider myself as the promoter of freedom.

The last sound that came through the phone was the crying of Rafiq.


Upon reaching his appointment, Kamrul found the door unlocked. He had not married yet, so he always locks it whenever he goes out. He goes inside the house and turns on the lights. What he saw in front of him made him freeze in place. Rafiqul Islam, his dead co-worker, was sitting on the sofa, smiling unnaturally. Rafiq or whatever it was, asked him,

        - Hello, Kamrul. How are you?
